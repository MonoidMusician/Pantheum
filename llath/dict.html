Ļaþ Roo English definition/translation Search by Ļaþ Search by English —oot— Root Root ≡ ẖ The fundamental Llath word; it means many things depending on context. From this are derived all other words.

When combined with a case ending, it becomes a noun that indicates something that typically carries out the action of the case. For example, with the ergative case ending “j”, “ẖj” means a doer; using the instrumental, “ẖu” means a tool, utility. × ≡ ẇ The question word, equivalent to English wh, Latin qu, PIE kʷ. Closely related to ẖ in its meaning of stems. × ≡ ļ This word generally refers to the act of written communication. Signed stems introduce the notion of truth, as in asserting that a statement is true or false. This is an extension of the zero-stem’s idea of existence; a statement that exists is “true”, otherwise “false”. × ≡ ĺ This word is very closely related to ļ, adding however the significance of verbal, rather than visual, speaking and talking. × ≡ ś This word generally signifies motion, the physical continuous change in location. Positive stems show motion towards a goal (i.e. progress) – culminating in finally reaching it – while negative stems show retrogression away from the goal. × ≡ ŝ The neutral stem of this word is typically used only in conditional or interrogative contexts, while the positive stem signifies revealing, and the negative stem the opposite: hiding. × ≡ d The root of existentialism! Verbs can form three stems from a root: neutral {ā}, positive {j}, negative {w}; the former can be referred to as “unsigned”, the latter two as “signed”. Additional variations of this may occur: semipositive {e}, seminegative {o}, etc., through the full range [positive, neutral, negative] {j,ı,ē,ĕ,ā,ŏ,ō,u,w}. Additionally may be (occasionally) used the remaining vowels, viz. {ă,ø,y}. Stems may occur even for which no definitions are given.
